# Ashworth Interview Scripts — verbatim reference

Source:  Jim Ashworth, Managing Partner, Ashworth Tomerlin & Associates —
"Interview Success Guide" Parts 1 & 2 and the March 10, 2026 workshop.  Jim frames
these as the **four areas of the interview that determine whether you get hired**,
on the premise that you are competing with two or three equally-qualified
candidates and your handling of these moments is the tiebreaker.

When you coach the user, keep their own facts and accomplishments; use these
scripts to shape *structure and framing*, not to invent content.  Quoted lines
below are Jim's exact wording — preserve it when the user wants his words.

---

## Area 1 — Reason for leaving

**The problem.**  Most candidates answer in 8–18 seconds.  Too short invites
follow-up questions that usually turn negative.  Too long sounds like complaining
or defensiveness.

**The target.**  ~30–45 seconds, up to a minute.  Thorough enough to close off
follow-ups, short enough to stay positive.

**Structure (start positive → neutral reason → severance close):**

1. **Open positive.**  Jim's exact opener:
   > "I'm glad you asked because I loved working there…"
   (Equivalent: "I absolutely loved working there.")
2. **Neutral business reason.**  Briefly explain the circumstances — layoff,
   acquisition, downsizing, restructuring — without grievance.  Long enough to
   avoid follow-up questions, not a story.
3. **Severance close.**  Jim's exact closing line:
   > "…and I appreciate the fact that they rewarded me with a severance for my
   > contributions, and that's allowing me to be selective about my next
   > position."

**Why the severance close works.**  It implies the employer *valued* you — more
persuasive than reference letters — and reframes you as a selective, high-value
candidate rather than someone who was pushed out.

**Hard don'ts:**
- Never say **"let go"** — it reads as *terminated*, not laid off.
- Don't overcompensate with "I have great references" — it signals defensiveness.
- If the user's situation differs (e.g., they wound the company down and helped
  others get severance), position that stewardship as the value story instead.

## Area 2 — "Tell me about yourself" / "Walk me through your resume"

**The problem.**  Candidates think the employer literally wants a resume recital.
The employer has already read the resume several times.  Reciting it bores them
and makes them lose the thread of *why you fit* and why they brought you in.

**The fix — confirm priorities, then map to them.**  Ideally learn the role's two
or three priorities/issues in advance (from the recruiter, HR, or a prior-round
interviewer).  Jim's exact framing:

> "In listening to you (or in talking with…) it sounds like the main priorities
> are … Why don't I start with the jobs I've been in where I dealt with those
> priorities/issues and how I went about correcting them.  If it appears I'm
> skipping over jobs you want me to discuss further, please let me know, but I
> think it would be beneficial if I started with those."

**Coaching steps:**  help the user (1) identify the role's top 2–3 priorities,
(2) pick the experiences that best demonstrate solving exactly those, and
(3) deliver them as problem → action → result, not chronology.

## Area 3 — Compensation:  "What have you been making? / What do you need to make?"

**The problem.**  Candidates try to defer ("I'd rather not say / it depends"),
believing the employer is negotiating.  Deferring *scares* the employer — it
looks like there's a problem.  In reality the employer has a range and is just
checking that you fit it.

**The fix — lead with salary history + structure, then signal modest flexibility.**
Jim's exact script:

> "In my last position, I was making a base of ___ with a significant bonus,
> equity (if applicable) and excellent benefits.  While I've been told by peers
> in similar roles that in this market I should be able to improve my
> compensation, I'm also willing to be a little flexible in order to prove
> myself."

**Calibration logic:**
- Calibrate to any known range (from the recruiter, HR, or the job ad).  Example:
  if you earned $220K and the role pays $225–250K, you might say "a little south
  of two and a quarter" — or "a little north" — depending on fit.  Avoid vague
  non-answers that produce "goofball offers."
- Include **structure** (bonus, equity) without committing to exact variable
  amounts — some employers don't pay bonuses, and volunteering this prevents
  misalignment.
- **Transparency:**  if you trust the company, be forthright; the quality of their
  offer will tell you whether they're a good employer.
- **Don't be the one to introduce the posted range unprompted**, even though more
  LinkedIn listings now show ranges.
- **Counteroffers:**  set data-driven expectations to pre-empt them.  Accepting an
  offer that came out of a flawed or misaligned process often ends in a departure
  within ~90 days.

## Area 4 — Setting the tone & closing the interview

**Open with a sincere statement of interest.**  Most candidates never consider
their contribution to the interview's tone, which is best set by an early
statement of interest; stating *why* you're interested adds sincerity.  Jim's
exact opener:

> "Based on reading the job description (or talking with the recruiter, or talking
> to HR), this position appears to be a great fit for my experience and what I'm
> looking for."

**Close by signaling interest and offering to meet others.**  Jim's exact close:

> "As I said earlier, I'm very interested in this position.  So, not knowing how
> long you would need me, I blocked off the rest of the morning (afternoon) in
> case there is anyone else who would like me to meet while I'm here?"

## Cross-cutting topic — Age & energy

Jim's last seven placements were candidates in their sixties; many of his clients
care only about capability, not age or gender.  Employers care about **energy,
productivity, and maturity**, not birth year.

**Rules:**
- **Keep graduation dates on the resume.**  Omitting them now reads as hiding your
  age, prompting interviewers to mentally add 10–20 years.  (See
  `resume-standard.md`.)
- **Demonstrate energy through three behaviors:**
  1. **Pace** — don't trail behind the receptionist or on a tour; move with
     purpose.
  2. **Posture** — sit perpendicular, lean forward to make points, smile.
  3. **Proof points** — lightly signal stamina/drive ("first in, last out,"
     distance running) without overdoing it.
- **"How many more years do you want to work?"** — never give a number (the
  interviewer mentally halves it).  Use principled, truth-based answers:
  > "I've always loved what I do.  As long as I'm productive and in great health,
  > I plan to keep going."
  or "My clients won't let me stop."

## Coaching note on interviewer quality

Jim provides candidates these scripts partly because many interviewers are poor
and ask weak questions.  Coach the user to stay in control regardless of question
quality by using the priority-confirmation pivot (Area 2), the compensation lead
(Area 3), and the reason-for-leaving structure (Area 1).
