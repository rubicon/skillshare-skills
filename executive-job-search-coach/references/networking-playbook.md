# Ashworth Networking Playbook — connections, LinkedIn, recruiters

Source:  Jim Ashworth, March 10, 2026 workshop, and the "Strategic Job Search &
Interview Tactics" framework.

**Core premise.**  Landings come from three places:  (1) people who already
"know, love, and respect" you — former employers and colleagues; (2) recruiters;
and (3) LinkedIn.  Jim's data point:  a decade ago ~10% of placements came via
LinkedIn; now, excluding recruiters and existing relationships, ~98% of the people
being *found* are found by HR and recruiters on LinkedIn.  The room kept asking
"how do I make more connections" — Jim's answer is that connections do not come
from the networking meeting itself; they come from systematically working LinkedIn
and your existing relationships.

---

## 1.  Start with people who already know you (warm-first)

- Go to your **former companies**, oldest job first.  Put each company name into
  LinkedIn; the people who worked there surface.  Look for people you had a real
  relationship with — **including admins** (an admin who knows you can flag an
  opening to their executive).
- Reconnect with your **prior banker, attorney, and insurance agents**, especially
  if you've relocated — ask them for referrals to contacts in your new area.
- Ask warm contacts for **introductions to local contacts and to second-degree
  connections at target companies.**
- **Lead with "I need your help."**  Jim calls it the most powerful opener —
  people like to help and feel good doing it.

## 2.  Understand connection degrees & map warm paths

- **1st-degree:**  directly connected.
- **2nd-degree:**  connected to someone you're connected to (warm path — ask for
  the intro).
- **3rd-degree:**  everyone else.
- When you see an opening, **check for a 2nd-degree path** into that company before
  applying cold.

## 3.  Always use the note field on connection requests

- Only about **one in ten** people add a note.  Jim ignores connection requests
  from unknown senders who include no note.
- A personalized note **raises acceptance rates and protects your send limits**
  (see InMail economics below).
- Practical note pattern:  who you are → the relationship or shared context → the
  specific, small ask.

## 4.  Respect LinkedIn's send economics

- Free accounts get roughly **20 InMails** (messages to people you're *not*
  connected with).  If you send them and recipients don't respond/connect,
  **LinkedIn can freeze the account.**
- Recruiter licenses are expensive (Jim's firm pays several thousand dollars a
  month; ~100 InMails costs real money).
- **Implication for the user:**  be judicious.  Prioritize warm/2nd-degree
  outreach, always include a note, and don't burn InMails on cold sends that won't
  land.

## 5.  Optimize the profile for discoverability

- **Smiling, approachable photo** — it conveys approachability.
- **Put a LinkedIn hyperlink on the resume.**
- Consider professional profile optimization to rank higher in recruiter/HR
  searches.  (In the workshop Jim specifically recommends **Terry Sullivan /
  BuzzPro** for this and says you may use his name when reaching out.  Treat this
  as an optional, source-accurate referral, not a requirement.)
- **"Open to Work" banner:**  avoid it *unless* you're 9–12 months in with no
  traction.  The banner can attract low-quality recruiters and unapproved
  submissions.

## 6.  Align with recruiters — and qualify them

- Align with **3–5 recruiters who specialize in your function.**  Avoid exclusivity
  with any single recruiter; openings are limited.
- To avoid **"disappearing recruiter" syndrome**, ask upfront:
  - "What is your follow-up policy?  Should I assume it's a pass if I don't hear
    back in 3–5 days?"
  - "Are you working with HR or directly with the hiring manager?"
  - "Do you have an exclusive?  What's your track record with this client?"
- Recognize that some recruiters have neither an exclusive nor an approved
  opening; these questions reduce ambiguity and improve follow-through.

## Outreach drafting (how to help the user write messages)

When drafting outreach for the user, apply the rules above:

1. **Reconnect (warm):**  reference the shared history honestly, open with "I need
   your help," and make one specific, easy ask (a 20-minute call, an intro to a
   named person/company).  Keep it short.
2. **2nd-degree intro request (to the mutual):**  name the target person/company,
   say why it's a fit, and make the forward effortless (offer a blurb they can
   paste).
3. **Connection-request note (to someone new):**  who you are + context + small
   ask, in the note field — never blank.

Keep every draft truthful to the user's real history.  Ask for the specifics
(names, companies, shared history) rather than inventing them.

## The workshop's "first three things" (Jim's own answer)

If asked "what's the first thing I'd do?", Jim's sequence is:
1. **Get a good resume** (see `resume-standard.md`).
2. **Get your story down on why you left your last company** (see
   `ashworth-scripts.md`, Area 1).
3. **Optimize your LinkedIn profile.**

Plus the workshop's recurring civic note:  **do something good for someone else.**
